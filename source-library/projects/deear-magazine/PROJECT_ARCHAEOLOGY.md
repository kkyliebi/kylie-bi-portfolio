# Deear Magazine — Project Archaeology

> Review status: first editorial excavation pass based on the current canonical record. This document identifies what must be clarified before Deear can move beyond a competent editorial-design case and reveal a distinctive communication idea.

## Current Strength

Deear already demonstrates typography, hierarchy, sequencing, pacing, and multi-page consistency. It provides clear evidence that Kylie can organise content into a coherent reading system.

The project becomes much stronger if it can show that editorial design was used to interpret a specific cultural or emotional subject, not merely to style pages.

## What Is Still Too Generic

- The publication’s exact theme and editorial proposition are missing.
- The relationship among identity, migration, emotional memory, and queer storytelling appears in supporting records but is not yet verified in the canonical account.
- The intended reader, publication context, and tone of voice remain undefined.
- The current process description could apply to almost any magazine project.
- No substantive editorial decision is yet explained through evidence.
- Collaboration and ownership boundaries are unclear.

## Hidden Soul to Test

Possible deeper proposition:

> The magazine uses editorial rhythm and visual sequencing to create a reading space for identities and emotional experiences that are difficult to communicate through linear explanation alone.

This direction should only be retained if the original content and visual material support it.

## Questions for Kylie

1. What is Deear actually about in one precise sentence?
2. Where did the title “Deear” come from, and does its spelling carry meaning?
3. Was this one issue, a magazine concept, a collaborative publication, or part of a larger editorial platform?
4. Who was the intended reader?
5. Which themes were central: identity, migration, queer experience, memory, intimacy, culture, or something else?
6. What content already existed before you began designing?
7. Did you influence editorial selection, sequencing, titles, image choice, or only visual layout?
8. What was the most difficult content to organise, and why?
9. What grid, typographic, or pacing decision became the backbone of the publication?
10. Did different article types require different page families?
11. How did the design balance emotional atmosphere with readability?
12. Was the magazine printed? What were the dimensions, page count, paper, binding, and production constraints?
13. Was the work individual or collaborative, and what was your exact ownership?
14. Which three spreads best reveal the editorial system rather than only visual attractiveness?
15. What did you learn about how readers move through emotionally complex material?

## Evidence to Look For

- original brief and editorial statement
- table of contents or issue map
- full PDF or InDesign export
- content hierarchy and sequencing notes
- grid and typography tests
- image-selection or crop studies
- alternative layouts
- printed-object photographs
- collaborator and role records
- final presentation or critique notes

## Narrative Upgrade Target

The final case should answer:

1. What editorial subject required interpretation?
2. How was content classified and sequenced?
3. What visual rules formed the publication identity?
4. Which key design decisions shaped reading pace and emotional tone?
5. How did the finished object support the publication’s cultural or narrative purpose?
